Personality and Temperament


With its slender, elegant face, the Abyssinian cat closely resembles ancient Egyptian statues of cats. Distinctive ticked coats in a range of exotic colors are paired with large, expressive eyes, adding an air of mystery to the breed's look.Typically outgoing and energetic, with a love of being in the spotlight, the Abyssinian cat forms a strong bond with its household. Despite this, however, the breed has a reserved aspect to its personality. Abyssinian cats are known to be swift to vanish when visitors appear.



Nutrition Grooming Exercise Health

Nutrition

Like all cats, Abyssinians thrive on the consumption of high-quality food rich in protein, amino acids, vitamins, and minerals. While all cats will benefit from eating high-moisture foods, Abyssinian cats require more water than most. Serving a high-quality wet food may assist cats in consuming additional moisture.

Grooming

The Abyssinian cat is very short-haired, so you won't be spending a great deal of time grooming your pet. Gently brush or use a grooming glove once or twice a week to brush off loose hair and make your Aby look and feel their best. Few Abyssinian cats enjoy being restrained or held, so get them accustomed to having their nails clipped from an early age with patience and positive reinforcement. Consult with your veterinarian on some advice, or have a groomer or vet clip your cat's nails for you.

Exercise

Health

Abyssinian cats generally have good physical health, though they are stressed and anxious, especially in new circumstances. Beyond that, Abyssinian some cats also develop a few recognized disorders, such as an inherited form of anemia known as pyruvate kinase deficiency (PK deficiency), a degenerative eye condition known as progressive retinal atrophy (PRA), patellar luxation, and an inherited kidney disease known as renal amyloidosis. Abyssinians live very long lives. It is not rare for Abyssinians to live 14 to 17 years or longer, with some Abys living well into their 20s.


History


Abyssinians are one of the oldest cat breeds in existence. The most recent genetic research suggests the breed originated in Southeast Asia, somewhere on the coast of the Indian Ocean. This breed retains many hallmarks of the appearance of felis lybica, a wildcat that contributed its DNA to all domestic cats in existence today.


The modern Abyssinian cat is somewhat different than the original Abyssinian cat. Many of today's Abyssinian lines were created with Russian Blues, Burmese, and Siamese cats as foundation stock. Abyssinian cats were first recognized by England's Governing Council of the Cat Fancy in 1929. America's first Abyssinian cat litter was born in 1935. The breed is recognized by the Cat Fanciers' Association and The International Cat Association.




Did You Know?

Abyssinian cats were first introduced to Europe by way of Ethiopia. Ethiopia was historically known as Abyssinia, and this contributed to the breed's name.





Abyssinian Cat: Breed Information






COAT


The Abyssinian has a brief coat that is fine and silky in texture. The agouti pattern, the typical tabby ticked coat, has each individual hair banded with alternating darker and lighter colorings with up to four to six bands of ticking. On all Abyssinian cat colors, the hair tips are darkest, and usually exhibit faint patterning.


COLOR


The CFA breed standard provides four recognized Abyssinian cat coat colors: ruddy, cinnamon, blue, and fawn. Separate nose leather and paw pad color are associated with each coat color. The TICA breed standard defines six traditional colors (ruddy, cinnamon, chocolate, blue, fawn, and lilac) and six colors in the silver division: black-silver, cinnamon-silver, chocolate-silver, and diluted blue-silver, fawn-silver, and lilac-silver.



BODY


Abyssinians possess sleek, slender bodies with developed musculature. Overall, there is a look of athleticism and poise. The average weight for the male Abyssinian is 7 to 12 pounds; that for the female is slightly less, at 6 to 9 pounds.


HEAD


The head of the Abyssinian cat is a modified wedge, with minimal rounding along the brow, cheek, and profile. Cheekbone shading, shadowing, and dots on the whisker pads are typical. The muzzle is even, not square or pointed. Entire adult males will have jowls.


EYES


Abyssinians have gold, copper, green or hazel eyes, accompanied by dark lines that run from the eyes and eyebrows. Abyssinian eyes are large, expressive, and almond-shaped and are responsible for this breed's exotic look. Darker skin on the eyelids surrounds the outer rim of the eyes and is edged with a lighter colored zone.


EARS


An Abyssinian cat's ears are large, wide-set, moderately pointed, and cupped at the base.


LEGS & PAWS


The legs of the Abyssinian cat are long, slim, and muscular. The oval paws are relatively small and appear to almost give the effect of the cat being on tiptoes.


TAIL


Abyssinian cats have long tails that are thickest at the base and taper to a point at the end.



Did You Know?

All cats are intelligent, but Abyssinian cats are off the charts. Highly trainable, eager to please, and athletic, Abys love to learn tricks, particularly when yummy rewards are offered.
