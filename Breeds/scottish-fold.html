Personality and Temperament


With just one look at the lovely Scottish Fold cat, it's clear why this breed received its name! With their folded ears and lovely looks, these family-friendly cats are just adorable. Scottish Fold cats get along well with children and other pets, such as sociable cats and well-mannered dogs.

Scottish Fold cats are gentle and playful, although many other breeds are more rowdy. They are content to spend time simply enjoying your company, and they are likely to follow you around the house as you go about your regular routine.

If you're looking for a cat who enjoys conversing, choose a Scottish Fold. Some talkative breeds, such as the Siamese, are quite loud, whereas the Scottish Fold's voice is much lower. These cats may acquire a fascinating language, which they use to express themselves and converse with you about everything from the amount of food in their dish to the fact that it's time for you to get up in the morning. When you talk to a Scottish Fold, you can expect to hear a lot of chatter.



Nutrition Grooming Exercise Health

The Scottish Fold cat has no unique nutritional requirements; nonetheless, we recommend providing a high-quality food with a high protein content and low carbs. If you sell a commercial brand rather than fresh food, be sure the first ingredient is actual meat or fish.

Brush short-haired Scottish Fold once or twice a week to get rid of any stray hair. Long-haired Scottish Folds require more frequent grooming‚Äîdaily or every other day‚Äîto avoid tangles and matting.

If your cat's ears are firmly folded, you may need to help him keep them clean and prevent accumulation. At least once or twice a week, gently clean beneath the folds with a cotton ball saturated with a 50-50 mixture of warm water and vinegar. Never use cotton swabs to clean the inner of your cat's ears; you could accidentally hurt their eardrum. If wax and debris appear to be piling up inside the ear and beyond the reach of cotton balls, contact your veterinarian.

Other critical grooming practices include nail clipping (weekly to every other week) and toothbrushing (daily). Teach your kitty to accept this form of handling from an early age to avoid problems later on.

The Scottish Fold cat enjoys playing, especially with at least one friend. If you have two cats, you may expect them to run, jump, and climb together. If it's just you and your cat, they'll enjoy any opportunity for interactive play.

Scottish Folds, like other cats, enjoy window seats, cat trees, scratching posts, puzzle toys, and common household items such as cardboard bags and boxes. The more options you provide for enriching your cat's environment, the happy they will be.

Later in life, your Scottish Fold may want additional encouragement to engage in active play periods in between naps. Keep an eye on their weight and, if feasible, raise their exercise level, since obesity can exacerbate joint problems and other illnesses.

When Scottish Fold cats with folded ears mate, the dominant gene that causes their ears to flop forward might induce skeletal malformations. As a result, prudent breeders combine one Scottish Fold with folded ears with a straight-eared Scottish Fold. Breeders can also outcross to one of two breeds: American Shorthair or British Shorthair.

The genetic abnormality that generates the breed's folded ears can also cause serious abnormalities in some cats' bones and cartilage, particularly in the ankle, knee, and tail joints. The illness, known as osteochondrodysplasia, is more likely to arise when cats have two copies of the defective gene rather than one, which may merely result in folded ears.

Some Scottish Fold cats are also predisposed to developing polycystic kidney disease and hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (a kind of cardiac disease), which are common in many otherwise healthy cat breeds.


History


The first Scottish Fold was a white barn cat named Susie. Susie, like many cats that pique breeders' interest, has a distinctive hereditary trait: folded ears caused by softening cartilage, which prevented the ears from standing up like a typical cat's ears.

Susie was contentedly collecting mice on a farm in Tayside, Scotland, when she attracted the attention of a shepherd named William Ross. In 1961, he and his wife Mary purchased one of Susie's female kittens from a litter sired by a local tomcat with regular, stand up ears. They named the kitten Snooks, and when she reached adulthood, she mated with a British Shorthair and produced her own litter.

Ross registered the Scottish Fold cat breed with the UK's Governing Council of the Cat Fancy (GCCF) in 1966, after some of the resulting kittens had folded ears, giving the new hybrid the name "lop-eared cats."




Did You Know?

All Scottish Fold cats are born with straight ears - the fold begins to develop when they're about 3-4 weeks old.

The famous singer Taylor Swift has two Scottish Fold cats named Meredith Grey and Olivia Benson.





Scottish Fold Cat: Breed Information






BODY


The body is circular and cushioned. Males may be considerably larger than females.


HEAD


The head is nicely shaped, with round whisker pads and large cheeks. The chin and jawbone are firm. Males may have substantial jowliness.


EYES


The large, well-rounded eyes are separated by a broad nose. The eyes have a pleasant expression and a wide-open look. Eye color connects to coat color.


EARS


The Scottish Fold cat's ears are small, folded forward and downward, and positioned to reveal the head's rounded cranial shape.


LEGS & PAWS


The legs are sturdy and proportioned to the body. The feet are well-rounded.


TAIL


The Scottish Fold cat has a medium to long tail that is proportional to its size. The tail tapers and has a rounded tip, rather than a whippy shape.


COAT


The coat might be long or short. Short-hair Scottish Fold cats have a dense, plush coat with a silky feel, and their hair stands out from the body. Scottish Fold cats have a complete coat that includes toe tufts, a ruff, britches, ear decorations, and a prominent tail plume. Long-haired cats can have short facial hair. Long-haired Scottish Folds are also known as Highland Fold cats.


COLOR


The Scottish fold can be any color or pattern. Buttons and lockets are allowed. The color of the nose leather and paw pads corresponds to the coat color.



Did You Know?

Scottish Folds often sit in a "Buddha position" with their legs stretched out and paws on their belly, which adds to their already adorable appearance.

The gene that causes the ear fold is the same one that can cause cartilage and bone problems, which is why responsible breeding practices are so important.
