Personality and Temperament


The Persian cat's thick, silky coat is just one of its many appealing features. Persian cats provide joy to their households with their loving nature and delicate but lively activities.

If you're searching for a cat that doesn't put on great athletic shows, the Persian may be the breed for you. These cats enjoy to recline, especially as they reach adulthood. Perhaps this is why they are sometimes referred to as furry furniture. Don't be deceived by this reputation. These cats have a lively streak, especially when their owners are interested in engaging in the fun.

Persians are peaceful and well-behaved pets who do not get into too much mischief. They are not as fond of opening drawers and cabinets as their Oriental counterparts, and unlike certain other breeds such as Siamese and Savannahs, Persians do not enjoy walking on leashes or playing fetch. However, they are intelligent cats who can learn your routine, greet you, and, of course, curl up for snuggles as part of their daily routine.



Nutrition Grooming Exercise Health

Nutrition

Because Persian cats are prone to obesity, it is critical to feed them a high-protein, low-carbohydrate diet rich in real meat or fish. Consider eating a diet with extra Omega fatty acids to improve skin and coat health.

Grooming

The Persian cat requires daily cleaning to avoid tangles in its ultra-fine undercoat, which can eventually produce unpleasant mats. Brushing and combing a Persian cat is not an easy chore. Comb down to the skin rather than brushing along the top of the coat to avoid missing tangles. This is a time-consuming process, so some Persian cat owners choose to shave their cats' bellies or clip the entire coat into a lion cut for ease of upkeep.

Persian cats also have facial wrinkles that need to be cleaned once or twice a day, depending on how much eye discharge the cat produces. In addition, brush your Persian cat's teeth every day and trim her nails every few weeks.

Exercise

Persian cats require activity, but be careful not to overdo it, especially in warm weather. Have fun with feather wands and lasers, but keep the pace slow and take a break if your Persian cat begins to pant or has shortness of breath.

Persians adore cat towers and window shelves that give them a fantastic view of everything going on in the house and neighborhood, and they also enjoy sinking their claws into scratchers. Persian cats, like other cats, enjoy playing with toys such as catnip mice, fascinating balls, and little plush animals.

Health

The Persian cat breed is brachycephalic, meaning it has a push-in face. The Persian's flattened face is associated with several known health concerns, such as shortness of breath and sweating during activity or in warm weather. Other health difficulties connected with flat-faced breeds include an extended soft palate, constricted trachea, stenotic nares, and everted laryngeal saccules. All brachycephalic cat breeds, including Persians, are prone to allergies.

Other common Persian health problems include frequent eye injuries, dental disease and malocclusion (packed and misaligned teeth), cancer, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, and polycystic kidney disease (PKD). PKD is an inherited condition that is common in the Persian breed and similar breeds such as the Himalayan and Exotic Shorthair. Fortunately, a genetic test is available, allowing breeders to avoid passing this terrible disease on to future generations of kittens.


History

Pietro della Valle, an Italian musician and explorer, observed Persian cats from Persia's Khorazan region in the 1600s, observing that the majority had long, silky, gray coats. His manuscript, Voyages de Pietro della Valle, states that the cats traveled to Persia from India with Portuguese sailors.

With regular travel came more imports: Persian and Angora cats made their way to France and England. In England, they were known as French cats, and they immediately gained popularity due to their distinct appearance and pleasant demeanor.

Persian Cat: Breed Information






BODY


The Persian is well-rounded and balanced, with a cobby body style and strong muscle tone. Most Persians are medium to large in stature, with males weighing more than females.


HEAD


The head is huge and spherical, and it rests on a short neck. When viewed in profile, the Persian cat's forehead, nose, and chin appear to be vertically aligned, with the eyes standing out.


EYES


The eyes are huge, round, and full, with bright colors. The Persian cat's eye color complements the coat color.


EARS


The ears are tiny and have a modest forward inclination. The points are rounded, and the furnishings are abundant.


LEGS & PAWS


The legs are short and robust, giving the image of strength. The paws are round and feature noticeable tufts between the toes.


TAIL


The tail is small yet well-proportioned, providing balance to the cat's body. Hair creates a full brush impression.


COAT


The coat is long, thick, and fine-textured. A prominent ruff that expands to form a frill between the front legs is ideal.


COLOR


Persian cats come in almost every color and pattern imaginable. Some registries differentiate Himalayans (colorpoint Persians) from other hues and patterns. The nose leather and paw pad colors complement the coat color.



Did You Know?

Persian cats were favored by royalty, including Queen Victoria who owned several blue Persians, helping to popularize the breed in the 19th century.

The Persian cat's flat face (brachycephalic) is a relatively modern development, as early Persians had more pronounced muzzles.
