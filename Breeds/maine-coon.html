Personality and Temperament


The Maine Coon cat stands out for its bold features, thick, luxuriant hair, and wonderfully sociable disposition. These adorable cats love to play, but they also like taking a well-deserved nap when the mood strikes. Maine Coon cats frequently snuggle up next to their favorite persons, which may be very beneficial on chilly evenings!

The Maine Coon's purr is wonderfully expressive and may be heard several feet away. Their vocalizations are remarkably modest for such large cats, yet their vocabulary is broad, including chirps and meows.

The Maine Coon, one of the largest cat breeds in existence today and one of the most popular breeds globally, has a heart that matches its size. These cats adore humans and get along nicely with other pets. They are good for families since they value children. When you meet a Maine Coon cat, you'll understand why they're known as the gentle giants of the feline world.



Nutrition Grooming Exercise Health

Maine Coon cats have no specific nutritional requirements. However, it is important to note that these giant cats require a high-protein diet, and their daily calorie needs can be far higher than those of a smaller cat. We recommend feeding your Maine Coon cat a fresh food or using a high-quality commercial brand with actual fish or meat as the first ingredient.

You may need to cut back on your Maine Coon's diet as he ages to prevent him from gaining weight. Offering an age-appropriate diet is one of the most effective strategies to ensure your cat's good health throughout their golden years.

The Maine Coon has a thick, dense double coat over a silky smooth undercoat. To avoid matting, the coat should be thoroughly brushed at least twice or three times every week. Some Maine Coons can require daily brushing. Your cat will appreciate the regular grooming sessions and see them as another way to bond.

Teach your Maine Coon to accept nail clipping at an early age. Keeping your Maine Coon cat's claws clipped will help protect your furniture, clothing, and skin.

Also, consider educating your Maine Coon to let you brush their teeth. Brushing your cat's teeth on a daily basis at home is the most effective approach to keep her teeth healthy and contribute to her general health. Daily teeth brushing can also help reduce the need for professional dental cleanings.

Maine Coon cats are energetic, but they also enjoy resting. Excessive inactivity can lead to obesity over time. Encourage your Maine Coon cat to play with feather wands and fishing pole toys, tossed balls and fuzzy mice, and laser pointers (but never flash the light in their eyes, and avoid frustration by giving them a real toy to "catch" at the end of the game).

If feasible, teach your Maine Coon to walk with a leash and a sturdy harness. The more active your cat is, the better their health will be in the long run.

Maine Coon cats are normally quite healthy. Most pedigreed cats have genetically connected health issues that are exclusive to their breed. The most prevalent conditions in the Maine Coon are feline hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, hip dysplasia, and spinal muscular atrophy. Reputable breeders test adult cats before breeding them, reducing the risk that these issues would be passed on to their kittens.


History


As you might expect, the Maine Coon cat is endemic to the state of Maine. This breed, which is native to the United States, most likely evolved in the 1850s when long-haired cats were transported to America and mated with local short-haired cats. The end result was a strong, robust, huge cat with exceptional hunting abilities, a thick, shaggy coat, and a long, flowing tail.

The breed was showcased at cat shows throughout the late nineteenth century. Farmers who cherished these cats for their exceptional ability to keep rats out of barns and outbuildings conducted their own competition, the Maine State Champion Coon Cat contest, at the Skowhegan Fair.

Maine Coon cats became less popular in the early twentieth century when new exotic long-haired cat breeds such as Persians arrived in the United States. The breed deteriorated until the 1950s, and some believe the Maine Coon may have been extinct.




Did You Know?

The Maine Coon is one of the largest domestic cat breeds, with males often weighing 20 pounds or more.

Maine Coons are known as "gentle giants" due to their large size and sweet, affectionate nature.





Maine Coon Cat: Breed Information






BODY


Maine Coon cats are strong, well-proportioned, and rectangular, giving the appearance of general balance. Male Maine Coons are usually larger than females. It is common for a male to weigh at least 20 pounds.


HEAD


The medium-width head is slightly longer than it is wide. The medium-length muzzle has a distinct square form. The chin is powerful and firm, and in profile, the chin depth appears squared, resulting in a 90¬∞ angle.


EYES


The Maine Coon cat has huge, expressive oval-shaped eyes that lean slightly toward the outer base of the ears. The eye color complements the coat's hue.


EARS


The ears are huge, with broad bases that narrow to pointed tips. They are about one ear's width apart at the base and have plenty of furniture. Ear tufts are good.


LEGS & PAWS


The legs are robust and medium in length, with good proportions to the body. The forelegs are straight, and the back legs seem straight when viewed from behind. Maine Coon cats have broad, rounder paws that are heavily tufted.


TAIL


The long tail is larger at the base and narrows toward the end. A lush, flowing plume is preferred.


COAT


Maine Coon cats have a heavy, shaggy double coat that comprises of an undercoat and longer guard hairs on top. The coat has a silky feel and falls easily. The tail is long and flowing, and a visible rough on the chest is preferred.


COLOR


Maine Coon cats available in every color and pattern except chocolate, lavender, and Himalayan.
