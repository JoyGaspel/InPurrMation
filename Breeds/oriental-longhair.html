Personality and Temperament


Two characteristics that set the Oriental Longhair Cat apart are its silky, opulent coat and its gregarious, amiable disposition. These adorable and affectionate cats have a lot in common with their Oriental Shorthair cousins and other Oriental cats: they are lively, smart, and far more gregarious than the typical cat, and they have an innate need for attention.

You will have a lifelong friend if you bring an Oriental Longhair cat into your house. Nothing makes these cats happier than to follow their favorite person about, sticking their curious noses into everything from the shower to the refrigerator and tapping with their paws whenever something doesn't seem right.

In keeping with their Siamese ancestry, Oriental Longhairs are able to create an astounding vocabulary of meows, trills, and chirps in addition to using body language to communicate. These outgoing cats have strong opinions and enjoy having conversations.



Nutrition Grooming Exercise Health

Nutrition

Although they don't require any specific diet, Oriental Longhair cats do well on a high-protein diet that primarily consists of real meat or fish. To promote the health of your skin and coat, look for a brand that has included Omega fatty acids.

Grooming

When compared to Persians and other cats with extremely long hair, Oriental Longhairs appear positively short-haired due to their single-layer coat, which is more medium length than long. Brushing won't be necessary; just give it a once-over every day to help get rid of any loose hair and reduce shedding.

Giving your cat a grooming session is beneficial for both of you because regular brushing can help lessen the likelihood of hairball accumulation. In addition to being adorable, these kitties are very active. Maintaining regular toenail trims might help prevent damage to your furnishings. Finally, think about brushing your cat's teeth at least a couple of times every week.

Exercise

Oriental long-haired cats love to play. Providing a window seat or two, a good scratching post, and at least one tall cat tree in each room will help both them and you. These cats will jump and climb on every piece of furniture you own if you don't have these necessities and a well-stocked toy collection.

Health

In general, Oriental Longhair cats are in good health. Asthma, megaesophagus, feline lower urinary tract disease, dilated cardiomyopathy, hepatic amyloidosis, and progressive retinal atrophy are among the conditions for which their Siamese ancestry does raise the risk. A tiny proportion of Oriental Longhairs are born with crossed eyes, which is a defect that is not allowed in the show industry but is not a serious health issue.


History


Oriental Longhairs belong to a broader group known as the Oriental, which also includes Javanese, British Angora, Foreign Longhair, and Mandarin cats. In order to produce cats with the body type and disposition of a Siamese, as well as a range of coat colors and patterns, these cats were produced with Siamese cats serving as essential foundation members.

In the 1950s, the Oriental tale started in England. During World War II, when food shortages and continuous bombardment severely strained civilization, numerous cat breeds were driven to the verge of extinction. Breeders created new colors and patterns as they attempted to repair their foundations.

In addition to their Siamese ancestry, the Oriental Shorthair and Oriental Longhair share Russian Blue, Abyssinian, British Shorthair, and other domestic genetics that have contributed to the Siamese gene pool's expansion and improvement. The only thing that separates an Oriental cat from a modern Siamese cat, after all, is color.




Did You Know?

The Oriental Longhair is essentially a longhaired version of the Siamese cat, sharing the same elegant body type but with a medium-length coat.

These cats are known for their "talking" ability and will often carry on lengthy conversations with their owners using a wide range of vocalizations.

COAT


Oriental Longhair cats have a medium-long coat with no downy undercoat. The fur should lie close to the body, making it appear shorter than it is. The longest hairs should be on the tail.


COLOR


A wide variety of colors and patterns are permissible.



BODY


The body is long, thin, and tubular. Oriental Longhair cats should have a slim and graceful appearance.


HEAD


The head is trapezoidal, with a wedge-shaped muzzle and a long, straight nose that results in an extended profile.


EYES

The eyes should be medium-sized and almond-shaped. They should tilt slightly to follow the visual lines created by the head and ears. Green eyes are prevalent, while blue eyes are preferable in cats with pointed coloring. It's possible to come across odd eyes.


EARS


The Oriental Longhair cat should have broad ears that follow the triangle lines on the head. The ears should have a wide base and pointy tips.


LEGS & PAWS


The legs should be long and thin, with the hind legs slightly longer than the front legs. Paws should be small, dainty, and oval.


TAIL


The Oriental Longhair cat's tail should be long and slender, tapering to a sharp point. The hair on the tail should form an exquisite plume.
