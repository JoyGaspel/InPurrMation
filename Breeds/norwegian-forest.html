Personality and Temperament


The Norwegian Forest Cat's striking appearance and loving demeanor let it stand out from the crowd. This gorgeous long-haired cat breed is ancient, with high intelligence, excellent social skills, and a fondness for all of life's pleasures.

Even though the Norwegian Forest Cat hails from a chilly region, they enjoy being warm. They enjoy an indoor existence with plenty of soft, comfortable surfaces for regular naps. They also value their human relatives, but they show affection on their terms rather than yours.

The Norwegian Forest Cat may chose to cuddle in your lap or sleep on your pillow, but it does not love being picked up or held unless it is the cat's idea.



Nutrition Grooming Exercise Health

As a somewhat large breed, the Norwegian Forest Cat may require more calories per day than the ordinary cat, particularly if they are very active. These cats require a high-quality diet and thrive on high-protein foods containing real meat or fish as the main ingredient.

If you can provide fresh food, your Norwegian Forest Cat will undoubtedly appreciate it. In any case, choose food rich in omega fatty acids to help your pet's skin and maintain their coat looking and feeling its best.

The Norwegian Forest Cat has a thick double coat, but it requires significantly less maintenance than you might expect. Brushing or combing once a week is generally enough to avoid matting and keep the luxuriant coat in top condition.

Regular nail clipping and daily toothbrushing are also important grooming practices to consider. Both should be introduced at an early age.

The Norwegian Forest Cat enjoys reclining, but that doesn't make it a full couch potato. These huge, fluffy cats are naturally athletic, enjoying running, jumping, and climbing.

They have the remarkable ability to descend from their cat tree headfirst, and their size allows them to cover several feet in a single leap. Tall cat trees, large scratching posts, and a plethora of toys are all appropriate enrichment materials.

Norwegian Forest Cats have great health and are noted for their lifespan. It is not uncommon for one of these cats to live more than 15 years. Even yet, the Norwegian Forest Cat breed has a few documented health problems.

Although they do not happen frequently, it is crucial to be aware of their presence. Potential concerns include glycogen storage disorder type IV, which can be discovered in parents using a DNA test. Glycogen storage disorder type IV frequently causes kittens to be stillborn or live for fewer than six months.

Norwegian Forest Cats may also suffer from polycystic renal disease and hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.


History


The Norwegian Forest Cat is a natural breed, which means it evolved over time without purposeful interference or human manipulation. The Norwegian Forest Cat, also known as skogkatt in Norwegian, was brought to Norway hundreds, if not thousands, of years ago. Nobody knows for sure if they accompanied Turkish traders or were brought back by Viking raiders. What we do know is that Norwegian forest cats play a fascinating role in Norse mythology. The huge cats that pulled the goddess Freya's chariot, for example, were referred to as skogkatt.

In 1938, a Norwegian Forest Cat was displayed at a cat show. Soon after, enthusiasts banded together to join the Norwegian Forest Cat Club.

The Norwegian Forest Cat, like many other breeds, nearly became extinct during World War II; however, after the war, members of the club banded together to resuscitate the breed and catapult it back into popularity.

Norwegian Forest Cat: Breed Information






BODY


The body is robust and sturdy, with a reasonable length, a broad chest, and a wide (but not obese) waist. Males are often significantly larger than females.


HEAD


The head is an equilateral triangle, with a straight nose, a firm chin, and a muzzle that blends into the rest of the face without generating conspicuous whisker pads. Males may exhibit substantial jowling.


EYES


The eyes are huge, expressive, and almond-shaped. They are positioned at a modest angle, with the outer corner higher than the inner. Eyes compliment the coat and are typically green, gold, copper, or green-gold. Cats with white patterns, as well as solid white Norwegian Forest Cats, might have blue or strange eyes.


EARS


A Norwegian Forest Cat's ears are medium to large in size, with thick furnishings. Lynx tips are regarded as highly desirable but not essential.


LEGS & PAWS


The legs are medium length, with the rear legs being slightly longer than the forelegs. The legs are powerful and muscular, while the paws are huge and round. The front paws appear to "toe out," and each paw has tufting between the toes.


TAIL


The Norwegian Forest Cat should have a long, bushy tail. Ideally, the cat's tail is the same length as its body. The tail is thickest at the base and has plenty of plumage made up of longer guard hairs.


COAT


Norwegian Forest Cats have thick, double coats with water-resistant guard hairs, a dense, woolly undercoat, britches, and a bib. The undercoat is often shed in the summer.


COLOR


Almost all colors and patterns are permitted, with the exception of those indicating hybridization, such as sable, chocolate, lilac, lavender, cinnamon, fawn, colorpoint markings, and any combination of these colors with white. The nose leather and paw pads can be any color.
